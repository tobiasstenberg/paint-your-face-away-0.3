Conceptual README

Paint Your Face Away is an art project addressing the issues of privacy and surveillance concerning the data economy of faces online in association with the technology of facial recognition.

The project develops and makes accessible to the public a browser-based application of an online face painter that creates portrait pictures that can resist the technology and operation of face scraping that powers some facial recognition systems.



Web-scraping of faces

Do you have pictures of your face on some websites or social media platforms? If so, chances are your faces could have been “scraped” (algorithmically gathered)* and used to train facial recognition systems to identify you, possibly without your consent. This kind of practice has been already done socially to build real facial recognition systems. See relevant newsfeeds.

Paint your face away started in mid-2019 before the above application has become widely known to the public. Its initiation was rather speculative and in response to the technological possibility of automatically collecting a vast number of faces to build a dataset to train facial recognition systems.

Technologies for scraping faces have been around for a while. Labelled Faces in the Wild , was conducted using a face detection system to gather facial images on the web. In more recent cases, there have been some semi-open source tools available that collect facial images on social media such as Instagram and Facebook which skilled developers could use to build functioning face scrapers. A face scraper could be built by combining face detection, object recognition (to look for the categories of images that may contain faces), and the hashtag sorting of relevant categories (such as #selfie, #portrait, #face) in order to make the process more efficient for gathering a large number of faces. The biggest known database created by web scraping to date has 3 billion faces.



How the painter works

You are invited to use this tool to paint a picture of your face until the site’s face detection system cannot detect a face in the image any longer. The finished portraits could potentially provide the following effects depending on how you paint them:

  1. Face detection obfuscation (though the detection proofing tested only with this model used and not necessarily working with other models).
  2. Quasi-anonymisation (if the face and ears are thoroughly painted out).
  3. Potential misclassification of facial recognition systems and/or object classification systems (an experimental feature).

Also, please see Disclaimer.

The painter allows you to decide either your facial identity is opaquely painted out by layering the paint, or slightly more “translucent”. Meaning your facial features may not become completely painted out but the process could enmesh your biometric data with the useless noise of paint so that it could resist the data harvesting of faces potentially.



-> Why is the tool designed in this way?

The painter is both informed and inspired by the technology and concept of the adversarial attacks that are a technical term that relates to Machine Learning. It investigates various data manipulation methods to hack algorithmic categorisation of AI systems [1].

The painter uses some methods of the adversarial attacks and explores the juxtaposing potential of paint as something irrelevant for the biometric data economy of faces.

Furthermore, the finished portraits can be circulated to help spread the word about the issues of face scraping and the availability of this painter. The more biometrically ruined images we provide, the more contaminated the online harvesting ground of faces could become potentially. Though when you share your portraits, be sure to paint them thoroughly so that your portrait is not identifiable.



Disclaimer

This is primarily an art project, and the testing capacity of the functionalities are limited by means of research resources and scientific expertise compared to laboratory-run scientific research.

The painting tool does not always guarantee perfect anonymisation (for the tools of anonymisation using face-blur please see Anonymous Camera, Signal, and Image Scrubber).

The face detection proofing does not necessarily work on other systems.

Some cases of misclassification against facial recognition and object classification systems have been observed with some painted faces ( ex1 | ex2 | ex3 ), but these are experimental features and the effects are not laboratory proven.
